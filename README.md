# When does the money run out?
When does the increasing cost of everything, cause a family to simply run out of money? A look at wage 'growth' vs CPI/HLPI scenarios. It's bleak.

# New Zealand Cost of Living — Research & Analysis
*Compiled April 2026 · Sources: Stats NZ, RBNZ, Public Service Commission, interest.co.nz, MoneyHub NZ, NewsWire NZ*

---

## 1. Inflation & Wage Growth — The 5-Year Picture (2020–2025)

### CPI (Consumer Price Index)

Annual CPI ran at 3.9% in 2021, surged to 7.2% in 2022, then eased to 5.7% in 2023. It fell to 2.7% for the year to June 2025, then rose to 3.0% for the year to September 2025. The December 2025 quarter landed at 3.1% year-on-year, and Q1 2026 remained at 3.1% — back above the RBNZ's 1–3% target band.

### Wage Growth (LCI Adjusted)

The LCI adjusted rate is the RBNZ's preferred indicator of wage inflation, as it best reflects underlying inflationary pressure by tracking pay rates for a fixed set of jobs. Annual wage growth for the year to June 2025 was 2.8% for the public sector and 2.3% for the private sector. By December 2025, LCI wage growth had slowed to 2.0% year-on-year.

### The Key Story (2020–2025)

There is a clear squeeze-and-release pattern. During 2021–2023, inflation ran well ahead of wages — real purchasing power fell. Wages then partially caught up through 2023–2024, driven heavily by public sector bargaining settlements. But by late 2025 the situation was reversing again: inflation crept back to ~3.1% while wage growth slowed to just 2.0%, meaning real wages were once again going backwards.

## 2. What CPI Doesn't Capture

CPI has significant blind spots, particularly for costs that hit New Zealanders hardest.

### Excluded Entirely

- **House prices** — the single biggest cost for most households. CPI captures rental costs and a proxy called "housing costs for owner-occupiers" (rates, insurance, maintenance), but the actual purchase price of a house is completely excluded. NZ house prices roughly doubled between 2019 and 2022.
- **Mortgage interest rate changes** — when the OCR went from 0.25% to 5.5%, repayments on a $600k mortgage jumped by ~$2,000+/month. None of that shows in CPI.
- **Asset prices generally** — shares, investment property, KiwiSaver fund values.
- **Second-hand goods** — not tracked.

### Structurally Underweighted or Distorted

- **Housing costs for renters** — rent is in CPI, but the basket weighting lags behind reality. Rents rose ~25–30% over 2021–2024.
- **Insurance** — included, but premiums surged 30–50% in many categories (home, contents, car) since 2021, driven by reinsurance costs and climate risk repricing. CPI captures this slowly.
- **Council rates** — included but weighted modestly, even though they rose 10–15% annually in many councils.
- **Food basket composition** — CPI tracks a fixed basket, so it doesn't fully capture *shrinkflation* (same price, smaller package) or trading down to cheaper brands.

### Methodological Quirks

- **Geometric mean formula** — produces lower inflation figures than a simple average by assuming substitution behaviour (that people swap to cheaper options when prices rise). This smooths the number but may not reflect lived experience.
- **Quality adjustment** — if a TV gets a bigger screen for the same price, CPI records a price *fall*. This suppresses measured inflation in technology categories.
- **Owner-occupied housing (OOH)** — NZ uses a "net acquisitions" approach rather than a rental equivalent, which significantly dampens how much housing costs appear in the index.

### Practical Upshot

For a typical middle-income worker, actual cost pressure is almost certainly *worse* than headline CPI suggests — particularly if they are a renter (rent inflation beat CPI), have a mortgage (interest cost surge), are insured (premium spikes), or pay Auckland/Wellington rates.

## 3. The Household Living-Costs Price Index (HLPI)

The HLPI is a Stats NZ measure that corrects some of CPI's key blind spots. It includes mortgage interest payments, consumer credit interest, and hire purchase interest. It covers 13 household groups including mortgage holders, renters, beneficiaries, superannuitants, Māori households, and income quintiles.

### Why It Matters

The HLPI peaked at 8.2% in the 12 months to December 2022, driven primarily by mortgage interest costs — in that period, the average cost of interest payments rose 44.9%. Owner-occupiers were experiencing a full percentage point more inflation than the headline CPI number suggested.

### The 2025 Twist

By December 2025, the picture had reversed: HLPI all-households was just 2.2%, well below CPI at 3.1%, as OCR cuts drove falling mortgage interest payments. The 2.4% September 2025 HLPI reading was largely due to a 15.4% drop in mortgage interest payments.

In the three years since December 2021, interest payments increased 104%, while the HLPI all-groups increased 19.3% — compared to CPI's 14.7% over the same period. That's a substantially higher cost baseline to drag forward.

### The Superannuitant Exception

Even as the headline HLPI fell, superannuitant households recorded the highest living-cost inflation at 3.8% in December 2025, driven by local authority rates rising 8.8%, electricity up 12.1%, and health insurance up 20.3%. The "average household" HLPI masks very different experiences depending on life stage and tenure type.

## 4. Single-Income Model — $100k Salary, 2% Wage Growth, 15% Savings Rate

### Setup

- Starting salary: $100,000 gross (2020)
- Wage growth: 2% p.a. (flat assumption for initial model)
- Savings rate: 15% applied to 2020 net income, establishing the cost-of-living baseline
- Cost of living basket: grows with actual CPI annually (Stats NZ data to 2025, then projected)
- NZ income tax applied (pre and post July 2024 bracket reform); ACC levy 1.6%

### What the Model Shows

The 2020 net income after tax and ACC was approximately $72,000. At a 15% savings rate, the cost-of-living baseline in 2020 was ~$61,200.

By 2024, three consecutive years of inflation far outpacing wages had reduced the savings rate from 15% to approximately 3% — using real CPI data. The 2024 tax bracket reform provided a small one-off boost but doesn't alter the long-run trajectory.

At the default settings (2% wage growth, 3% ongoing CPI), the model's crossover — where cost of living exceeds net take-home pay — arrives around **2028–2029**.

### Tax Drag

Every dollar of wage growth nudges the earner slightly up the tax brackets, meaning after-tax wage growth is always less than the headline figure. This compounds the squeeze: at 2% nominal wage growth, real after-tax purchasing power grows even more slowly than the gross figure suggests.

### The Key Sensitivity

At 3.5% wage growth vs 3% CPI, the crossover extends well beyond the modelled window. The margin is that thin. A single percentage point of additional wage growth makes the difference between solvency and deficit by the end of the decade.

## 5. Two-Income Mortgage Model — $230k Gross, $550k Mortgage, 2yr Fixed Rate Cycle

### Setup

- Combined household gross: $230,000 ($115,000 each)
- Mortgage: $550,000 owner-occupier, P&I, 30-year term
- Fixed rate: 2-year refix cycle using actual prevailing market rates
- Income split equally; NZ tax applied per person
- Starting savings rate: 15% of 2020 net income

### The 2yr Fixed Rate History

| Period | Rate | Context |
|---|---|---|
| 2020–2021 | 3.0% | COVID emergency lows |
| 2022–2023 | 5.5% | OCR hiking cycle begins |
| 2024–2025 | 7.2% | Near-peak; 2yr rates peaked ~7.5% mid-2023 |
| 2026–2027 | 5.0% | OCR relief; rates ~4.95% by Q2 2025 |
| 2028+ | 5.5% (projected) | Banks forecast mild OCR rises |

### The Critical Shock — 2024 Refix

The 2024 refix at 7.2% was the single most damaging event in the model. It added approximately $12,000–$15,000 to annual mortgage costs compared to the 2022 fixing, on top of a cost-of-living basket that had already compounded 20%+ since 2020. The household savings rate collapsed from 15% in 2020 to approximately 2–3% by 2024.

The 2026 refix to 5.0% provides meaningful relief, but the household never recovers its original savings rate within the modelled period, because the non-mortgage cost baseline has permanently shifted upward.

### Finding

Even at $230,000 combined income — well above the NZ median — the combination of a peak mortgage refix and sustained CPI inflation essentially eliminates the savings buffer within four years. The household remains technically solvent (surplus doesn't go negative until the late 2020s at current assumptions) but is operating with almost no financial resilience.

## 6. The LCI Real Wage Lens

### What the LCI Is

The Labour Cost Index (adjusted) is the cleanest measure of wage change available. Unlike median salary statistics, which are influenced by workforce composition shifts (more professional roles, credential inflation, sector mix), the adjusted LCI tracks the **same job at the same quality and quantity of labour** over time. It excludes promotions, progressions, and bonuses. The RBNZ uses it as its primary wage inflation input for monetary policy.

### The 20-Year Picture (2005–2025)

| Year | Nominal wages (LCI, 1992=100) | CPI (1992=100) | Real wages |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2005 | 126.7 | 129.8 | 97.6 |
| 2008 | 139.7 | 142.8 | 97.8 |
| 2015 | 158.9 | 161.0 | 98.7 |
| 2020 | 174.7 | 173.3 | **100.8** |
| 2023 | — | — | **94.4** (trough) |
| 2024 | 200.2 | 210.1 | 95.3 |
| 2025 | 204.9 | 216.1 | 94.8 |

*Source: Stats NZ Labour Cost Index; Stats NZ CPI. Annual averages, 1992=100.*

**The real wage index at end-2025 (94.8) is lower than it was in 1992.** Nominal wages roughly doubled over 33 years. Consumer prices grew slightly faster. Real wages have never meaningfully exceeded their 1992 level — they briefly matched it only in 2019 and 2020.

### Annual LCI Rates (2020–2025) vs CPI

| Year | LCI nominal | CPI | Real wage change |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2020 | 2.0% | 1.7% | **+0.3%** |
| 2021 | 2.5% | 3.9% | **−1.4%** |
| 2022 | 3.5% | 7.2% | **−3.7%** |
| 2023 | 4.5% | 5.7% | **−1.2%** |
| 2024 | 3.5% | 2.5% | **+1.0%** |
| 2025 | 2.2% | 3.1% | **−0.9%** |

*Sources: Stats NZ LCI releases; Public Service Commission quarterly LCI technical reports.*

### What 2023 Actually Was

The 4.5% LCI reading in 2023 — which superficially looked like wages catching up — was almost entirely driven by the **Public Sector Pay Adjustment**, a one-off Cabinet-mandated correction. The private sector LCI remained at ~3.0%. The Pay Adjustment won't repeat, and its effect on annual growth figures has now fully washed out of the index.

### The Structural Problem

At the 20-year LCI average of ~2.4% nominal against a CPI average of ~2.6%, real wages shrink by approximately 0.2% per year in a "normal" environment — before any inflation shock. This is not a COVID story. Real wages were below their 1992 level in 30 of the 33 years since the LCI series began.

### Productivity Divergence

Over the same 33-year period, labour productivity rose substantially in every sector: goods-producing industries +18%, manufacturing +26%, services +60% (Stats NZ Industry Productivity Statistics). Workers produced substantially more per hour. They were not paid for it. The labour share of GDP declined from roughly 60% in the early 1980s to the 42–44% band seen since 2012.

### What Real Recovery Would Require

To genuinely arrest real wage erosion, LCI growth would need to consistently exceed CPI by at least 0.5–1.0 percentage points. At current CPI (~3.0–3.1%), that means LCI growth of at least 3.5–4.0% p.a. sustained — roughly double the current 2.2% reading, and well above the 20-year average.

## 7. Summary of Key Findings

1. **CPI understates lived inflation** for owner-occupiers. The HLPI is a more accurate measure for mortgage-holding households, and peaked nearly 1 percentage point above CPI during the 2022–2023 rate cycle.

2. **The 2021–2023 compression was severe and compounding.** Three years of real wage losses of −1.4%, −3.7%, and −1.2% respectively produced a cumulative real purchasing power decline that cannot be quickly recovered.

3. **The savings buffer is the canary.** A household at 15% savings rate in 2020 on $100k was at ~3% by 2024 on real CPI data — and already negative if using HLPI. Any unexpected expense immediately creates a deficit position.

4. **The 2024 mortgage refix was the decisive shock for owner-occupiers.** Moving from 5.5% to 7.2% on a $550k mortgage added ~$12,000–$15,000 to annual costs at the worst possible moment — when the non-mortgage cost basket had already risen 20%+.

5. **The LCI exposes a structural problem, not a cyclical one.** Real wages have been flat or negative for 33 years. The 2023 "catch-up" was a one-off public sector adjustment. Without sustained LCI growth of 3.5%+, real wages will continue to erode.

6. **At median incomes, the math simply doesn't work long-term.** The single-income $100k model crosses over to deficit by 2028–2029 at current trends. The dual-income $230k mortgage household survives longer but with essentially no savings buffer — one rate shock, one job loss, or one major unexpected expense away from deficit.
